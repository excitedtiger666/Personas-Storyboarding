User Experience: Amazon Pickups and Returns via Kiosk
Overview:

It is safe to say that Amazon has revolutionized the customer experience. With the advent of amazon prime, luxuries such as next-day shipping and convenient return policies have become a staple in providing the best customer experience. In this project, I explored how Amazon continues to push the boundary of consumer satisfaction by expanding its pickup and return policy to Amazon Hubs. Customers can now pick up and or return their packages at Amazon Hub locations. The pickup and return process is made possible by a kiosk machine. This project analyzes the user experience of the Amazon hub kiosk interface.

Kiosk:

The kiosk is attempting to make the return and pickup process at the Amazon hub simple and fast. Therefore, it makes sense that the kiosk has very few components. Its initial loading screen is simple and instructs the user to either present their code in the scanner below or to touch the screen to enter their code. The barcode can be found by visualizing the arrows around the interface. Upon scanning the barcode, the locker opens and the user can receive their package.

Observations:

To get at the efficacy of the kiosk, I observed users interacting with the interface. To limit the interference of my own bias, I attempted to get objective observations only. In about 20 minutes, the kiosk was used by around 15 people. Here are some observations I noted during this time:

  • All users observed received or returned their package in less than two minutes.
  • No users had trouble locating the interface as identified by a smooth gait towards the interface.
  • Users could fairly easily locate the barcode scanner.
  • There was some observed trouble with getting the barcode to be scanned on the first try.
  • After scanning the barcode, users could easily locate their locker - which consisted of their package and or a space to put their item. Every user intuitively knew to close the locker despite not being prompted by the interface.
Interviews:

Realizing that observations may be deceitful, I decided to interview certain users about their personal experiences interacting with the kiosk. The goal of interviewing was to get at the userâ€™s motivation, expectations, experience, and challenges using the interface.

Asking good questions, while may simple, was incredibly difficult. Challenges included grappling with using either more open-ended or close-ended questions and limiting leading questions and questions that are two parts. To make sure I was following good practice, I adopted many guidelines from Dillman, which include:

  1. Ask one question at a time.
  2. Use simple and familar words.
  3. Use as few words to pose the question.

Some integral questions that were posed are shown below (in parentheses highlights the Dilman guidelines used):

  1. Is this your first time using the Amazon return/pickup machine? (#2: machine vs kiosk)
  2. What are your general feelings about having to pick up/return amazon packages by this method? (#1: asking one question at a time)
    • What about interfacing with a machine and not a human?
  3. When you entered the store, did you know where the machine you needed was?
  4. How long did it take you to understand the first step from the loading screen?
  5. Did you know where to scan your barcode? (#3: use as few words as possible)
    • Was the scanner difficult or easy to use?
  6. After you received or placed your package, did you know what to do with the open locker?
  7. If there was one thing you could have changed about the machine, what would it be?
Responses:
  • Users reported no trouble locating the kiosk, appreciating how easy it was to locate.
  • Most users liked the layout of interacting with a machine and having a human on hand to answer questions if they needed help.
  • The scanner was noted by many as being the most difficult part of the process. However, users noted that this was minor and only required slight adjustments in either the positioning or the brightness on their phones.
  • The users all intuitively knew to close the locker despite not being prompted by the interface to do so.
  • All users enjoyed how quick the process was, appreciating how the machine didnâ€™t ask for much information or take too long to process their request.

Why would Amazon be interested in creating hubs where people can return or pick up their items? Who did they have in mind while designing such an experience and the interface that would be used? What edge cases did they account for? These questions are at the heart and soul of implementing a human-centered design.

In this section, we will dive into the different personas Amazon may have had in mind when creating this interface. Personas are user models that are represented as specific, individual human beings (Cooper). They are critical in accounting for the diverse array of humans that will be utilizing your interface. Human-centered design positions users at the forefront of the development process. Effective storyboarding is a great way to understand and empathize with the user on a more intimate level and thus putting them at the forefront of the design process. Let's bring Speedy Sam's persona to life by looking at a storyboard to understand his situation better.

Diving into Sam's story, we get a better understanding of why the Amazon Hub Interface would be used in the first place and why it was designed the way it was. Here is an incredibly busy user. The use of the interface for him is an errand and an extra nuisance to his already hectic life. Therefore, the interface must be simple and fast to use while ensuring that security is prioritized. Therefore, as discussed in the Kiosk section, Amazon's choice to incorporate very few components and make the design of the interface simple as possible makes complete sense. In the end, the result is a satisfied customer who successfully utilized the interface, leading to an easier day in his life- nothing better than that :)
